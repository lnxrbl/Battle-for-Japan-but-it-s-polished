# Battle-for-Japan-but-it-s-polished
Weathered, immersive aesthetic. Physical maps to pore over. Battle for Japan is an ALT icon–let's give it a fresh coat of paint.

TO PREFACE: This is a base game, not a bespoke lesson plan. For maximum flexibility, I have not included anything that caters to a specific unit or textbook. The implementation of content is up to the ALT. In my classes, I write prompts on the chalkboard and have teams answer via whiteboard.

WHAT MAKES THIS VERSION DIFFERENT?
Gone are the days of PowerPoint. Download the "Battle For Japan" folder (Drive/DB link below), then run the HTML file, either by double-clicking it or right-clicking and selecting "Open with..." the browser. (Aside: the decision for the map to be vertical might seem counterintuitive, but my students were largely unbothered. Worst case scenario, the ALT can zoom in/out as needed.) Just click on a prefecture and choose a color from the wheel. The scoreboard will automatically track points. Clicking and dragging will select multiple prefectures. Forgot a prefecture's name? Just hover the mouse over it. To completely wipe the board, click the "Reset" button at the bottom of the page.

Additionally, each team gets something tactile: a map printout (attached below) to use as reference. Maybe this seems unnecessary, but I found that having more than just a screen to stare at often excited students, encouraging engagement. Be sure to laminate it and cut off those nasty corners!

HOW DO YOU PLAY?
The formula is largely the same as most variations:
1) Split everyone into teams, providing each team with a map, whiteboard, marker, and eraser/wipes. Teams choose their color and the game begins.
2) Each Turn, the ALT poses a question/prompt for the class to answer, then sets a timer (if the classroom doesn't have one, the ALT/JTE/homeroom teacher can count).
3) Once time is up, each team puts their whiteboard in the air. On it should be written two things: the answer to the question, and the name and/or number (see the map) of the prefecture they wish to capture.
4) Note than on Turn 1, teams can choose anywhere to capture, but after that they may only choose prefectures adjacent to their own.
5) If a team's answer is wrong, nothing happens. If they answer correctly, they capture the prefecture. Should two or more teams choose the same prefecture, they must compete to win it. This can be as simple as janken or something more involved like a sudden death prompt. The winner takes the prefecture, and the loser(s) makes no progress.
6) At some point during the game, introduce the ability to capture certain non-adjacent prefectures via Boats, the dotted blue lines connecting:
-Hokkaido(1) to Aomori(2), Fukushima(8), and Kyoto(29)
-Fukushima(8) to Mie(25)
-Kyoto(29) to Hokkaido(1) and Fukuoka(40)
-Hyogo(30) to Tokushima(38)
-Ehime(36) to Oita(41)
-Okinawa(47) to Kagoshima(43)
7) At some point during the game, introduce the ability to capture already occupied prefectures. There are different ways to implement this mechanic, with the simplest being just another round of janken. A more nuanced version: if a team answers wrong, anyone attempting to capture their prefecture automatically wins.
8) Once all prefectures are captured, the ALT can end the game or continue allowing teams to try to steal each other's territory, depending on how many questions/prompts are on hand. In the case of a tie, the leaders enter sudden death.
